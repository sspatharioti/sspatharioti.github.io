Abstract Advisor's Statement

The first quarter of the 21st century is currently being shaped by the rapid rise of environmental disasters across the globe, from naturally occurring events, such as hurricanes, floods caused by severe rainfalls, earthquakes, toxic algae disrupting natural habitats, as well as industrial disasters such as the Deepwater Horizon Spill. These events are projected to become more intense in the following years due to the effects of climate change and continuously rising water levels, which calls attention to designing more effective disaster response and monitoring operations.

With government resources becoming increasingly limited to respond to the plethora of events happening annually, smaller organizations, such as local non-profits, are tasked with monitoring and advocating for the affected areas in the aftermath of a disaster. This is often achieved by analyzing high volumes of aerial imagery made available from private or public survey flights. As some types of assessments may not be feasible using computer vision algorithms, these organizations rely heavily on attracting volunteers to sort through and label the data. However, the repetitive and tedious nature of such tasks results in the majority of volunteers disengaging early on in the process.

In this work, we propose to build an open-source crowdsourcing platform called Cartoscope, that aims to connect smaller organizations with volunteers for performing image analysis for disaster response, using a range of different tools and mechanisms to tackle volunteer disengagement. The platform is comprised of three different image labeling tools: a classic image labeling interface, a web image matching game called Tile-o-Scope Grid and an Augmented Reality (AR) mobile application for image matching called Tile-o-Scope AR. All three tools utilize different mechanisms for increasing volunteer engagement and retention, focusing on two main axes. The first axis concerns incorporating elements of task variety to the image labeling process. The second axis utilizes the interplay between competition and collaboration in image matching games to foster deeper engagement with the project objectives. Our implementations are evaluated using user studies, as well as running experiments on crowdsourcing marketplaces such as Amazon Mechanical Turk.
