Abstract About the Committee

Crowdsourcing is increasingly becoming a viable solution for rapidly analyzing large amounts of data that require human judgment and generating meaningful results. Within this area, image labeling tasks are gaining wide adoption across different disciplines, as a popular, cost-effective solution for performing image analysis, with applications ranging from Machine Learning training and citizen science, to disaster response and monitoring, and environmental justice. However, the repetitive and tedious nature of such tasks pose a significant challenge for project creators to retain participants, as they become disengaged early in the process. This can not only lead to less diverse outputs, due to small groups doing most of the work, but also poses a threat for smaller projects to gain traction and collect enough data, or succeed in their awareness and volunteer recruitment efforts.

This thesis will tackle crowd disengagement in image labeling tasks by focusing on two core axes: task variety, and co-location. More specifically, the first part of the thesis will focus on how can task variety positively impact performance in crowdsourced image labeling, as well as on designing variable and adaptive task variety mechanisms for increasing participant retention and throughput. The second part of the thesis will examine how introducing co-location in image labeling, via a multi-person tabletop image labeling toolkit, can lead to deeper engagement and discussions from participants. Within this co-located setting, the interplay of collaboration and competition will be explored in order to identify effective mechanics and rulesets that can achieve high engagement levels across participants with different backgrounds.

These research directions will be addressed by building an open-source crowdsourcing framework called Cartoscope. The framework is comprised of three different image labeling tools: 1) a classic image labeling interface called Cartoscope, 2) a web image matching game called Tile-o-Scope Grid and 3) an Augmented Reality (AR) tabletop image labeling toolkit called Tile-o-Scope AR. Different task variety and co-location mechanisms will be deployed on all three interfaces with a goal of increasing participant engagement and retention. These implementations will be evaluated using user studies, as well as running experiments on crowdsourcing marketplaces such as Amazon Mechanical Turk.
